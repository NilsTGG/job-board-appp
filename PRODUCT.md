# Product

## Register

product

## Users
Minecraft players who need reliable help with in-game logistics, fulfillment, and recovery. They typically come in with a practical job to get done: move items between bases, transport villagers, recover gear or inventory, place orders from partner shops, or outsource repetitive tasks they do not want to handle themselves.

They are operating in a game-native context, often coordinating through Discord while balancing urgency, trust, and payment in diamond terms. They want the interface to feel easy to use, competent, and worth trusting with coordinates, order details, and time-sensitive requests.

## Product Purpose
Because You Won't™ exists to turn Minecraft logistics into a clear, trustworthy service flow. It gives players a structured way to request services, estimate pricing, browse partner-shop inventory, and place orders without awkward back-and-forth or unclear expectations.

Success looks like two things working together: service requests that feel fast, legible, and professional, and marketplace orders that feel simple, credible, and operationally grounded. The product should reduce friction for both high-intent service bookings and item purchases while preserving the brand's distinct voice.

## Brand Personality
Playful, competent, irreverent.

The voice should feel unmistakably Minecraft-native, witty, and self-aware, but never sloppy or unserious. Humor is welcome when it sharpens memorability or makes the experience feel more human. Operational surfaces should still communicate trust, clarity, and follow-through. The emotional target is confidence with personality: users should feel that the service is run by someone capable, experienced, and fun to buy from.

## Anti-references
- Sterile, interchangeable SaaS UI that feels like a generic startup template with no voice or subcultural identity.
- Chaotic gamer-shop aesthetics that rely on loud neon overload, low-trust meme energy, clutter, or juvenile visual noise.
- Any experience that makes core actions feel gimmicky, confusing, or unserious when users are trying to submit orders, share coordinates, or evaluate trust.

## Design Principles
1. **Make operational trust visible.** Pricing, availability, process, and expectations should feel explicit and easy to verify.
2. **Let the brand speak without obscuring the task.** Personality should sharpen recall and delight, not compete with ordering, requesting, or decision-making.
3. **Design for dual intent.** The product must serve both service-booking and marketplace-ordering flows without making either feel secondary or bolted on.
4. **Stay native to the Minecraft economy and culture.** Language, payment framing, and workflow cues should feel legible to experienced players without drifting into parody.
5. **Reduce friction at moments of commitment.** Forms, estimates, cart actions, checkout, and confirmation states should feel calm, clear, and trustworthy.

## Accessibility & Inclusion
Target WCAG AA across the experience. Preserve strong readability, visible focus states, keyboard accessibility, and sensible contrast in all critical flows. Respect reduced-motion preferences by treating motion as enhancement rather than dependency. Humor and tone should never reduce clarity of labels, policies, or transactional states.
